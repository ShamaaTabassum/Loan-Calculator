    Why you should use a refinance calculator

    The business loan warrior refinance calculator aids in evaluating the value of replacing your current loan with a new one. By inputting a few details, the calculator determines if the benefits of refinancing outweigh the associated costs, helping you make an informed decision.

    How to use a mortgage refinance calculator

    For optimal results from the refinance calculator, gather essential details about your existing mortgage. Additionally, provide information about the new mortgage you intend to apply for.

    Your current mortgage information

    To utilize the refinance calculator effectively, input the following details:

    1. Original amount: Enter the initial borrowed amount of the loan you are refinancing.
    2. Loan Start Date: Provide the start date of your current loan, typically found on your monthly mortgage statement.
    3. Loan term: Select either a 30-year or 15-year term for the refinanced loan.
    4. Interest rate:Input your current interest rate, which can also be found on your current mortgage statement.

    Your new mortgage information

    1. Loan term:Choose between a 30- or 15-year term
    2. Interest rate: Add the interest rate you’d like. Check current refinance rates for more precise numbers.
    3. Lender & Title Fees: Our calculator automatically assumes refinance closing costs equal to 2% of your new loan amount — actual costs may range between 2% and 6% of your loan amount.
    4. Length of Ownership:Make sure you enter how long you plan to live in your if it’s more than five years — if you don’t, your refinance breakeven point won’t be accurate..

    Understanding the refinance calculator break-even point

    The refinance break-even point represents the timeframe required to recover the closing costs associated with your refinance. For instance, if refinancing allows you to save $200 per month, but you incur $6,000 in costs, your break-even point would be 30 months ($6,000 divided by $200 equals 30 months). If you intend to remain in the home for at least 30 months, refinancing would result in savings. However, if your stay is shorter, it may not be financially beneficial to pursue a refinance.

    Our refinance calculator will provide two recommendations based on the breakeven point results:

    1. Refinance makes sense. You’ll breakeven before you plan on moving.This simply means you’ll save more than the refinance costs, and the refinance is worth it.
    2. Refinancing makes sense if you plan to stay for more than “x” years. You’ll not break even before you plan on moving.If you get this warning, you may want to skip your refinance plans for now. The “X” will feature a number that tells you how long you need to stay in your home based on the information you provided.

    Mortgage Refinancing FAQs

    Q:How does how long you stay in a home affect your refinance decision?

    You may be tempted to just ignore the “Length of Ownership” field in your calculations and leave it pre-set to five years. However, before you spend thousands of dollars on closing costs, get your home appraisal and provide all the documentation you typically need to refinance, make sure you’ve given some thought to how much longer you’ll be in the home.
    Is it time to get a bigger home to support your growing family? Maybe it’s time to downsize your home now that the kids have flown the coop. Or it could be time to ditch city living for a home in the country. Pondering these questions before you refinance could save you time and money on something that won’t benefit you financially.

    Q:What are the different types of mortgage refinances?

    Conventional refinance loans. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac set the guidelines for the most popular loan type: conventional loans. You can avoid mortgage insurance with 20% equity in your home.
    FHA refinance loans. Homeowners with scores as low as 500 may qualify to refinance with an FHA loan. However, you’ll pay FHA mortgage insurance regardless of your equity amount.
    VA refinance loans. Eligible military borrowers may be able to borrow up to 100% of their home’s value with a VA rate-and-term refinance. VA borrowers can borrow 90% of their home’s worth with a VA cash-out refinance.
    USDA refinance loans. Borrowers in rural areas with current USDA loans can lower their payment, but don’t have a cash-out option.

    Q:How does a mortgage refinance work?

    Unless you’re eligible for a streamline refinance program like the FHA streamline or VA interest rate reduction refinance loan (IRRRL) the following steps best describe how to refinance a mortgage: 1. Pick your financial goal and loan program. This is important because any changes (like switching from a rate-and-term refinance to a cash-out refinance) could add extra hoops — and costs — to your refinance loan. The same is true for loan programs — choose a government-backed refinance loan if you need to refinance with bad credit, or a conventional loan if you have a high credit score and more than 20% equity in your home. 2. Shop for a lender and lock your rate. Once you’ve reviewed loan estimates and chosen the best lender for your needs, ask your lender for a mortgage rate lock. Mortgage rates change daily and your rate isn’t guaranteed until it’s locked in. 3. Gather your paperwork and get your home appraised. In most cases, you’ll need to provide current paysubs, W-2s and bank statements. You’ll also need information about the loan you’re paying off. Finally, spruce up your home for the home appraisal, unless you’re eligible for an appraisal waiver. 4. Finalize your closing disclosure and enjoy your savings. Once your loan is approved, you’ll receive a closing disclosure three business days before you sign. Review it and make sure the numbers are correct. If they are, sign your papers and you’re all done.

    Q:How do I find the best refinance rates?

    The answer is simple: shop around. LendingTree studies show that consumers who shop for a mortgage save thousands of dollars in interest charges.
