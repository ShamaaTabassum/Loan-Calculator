How to use our home affordability calculator

Achieving a manageable home budget hinges on two crucial factors: 1. The surplus funds available in your financial plan following expenses, and 2. The assessment by a mortgage provider regarding your affordability, which takes into account your earnings, debts, and savings for a down payment.

Gaining clarity on this distinction and utilizing a home affordability calculator to perform calculations will assist you in determining the actual amount of house you can afford.

Discover the secrets to determining your affordable home price with these four simple steps.

STEP 1: ENTER YOUR ANNUAL GROSS INCOME

Your gross income refers to your annual earnings before taxes or deductions. When using the mortgage affordability calculator, your gross income is divided by 12 to determine your monthly qualifying income.

STEP 2: ADD UP YOUR MONTHLY DEBT

Ensure to incorporate your monthly payments for student loans, credit cards, car loans, and any other expenses listed on your credit report. Lenders assess your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio by dividing your total monthly debt payments by your income, which is a crucial factor in determining your affordable home price.

STEP 3: PICK A DOWN PAYMENT

Your down payment is an initial payment made when purchasing a home. Generally, a higher down payment enables qualification for a higher-priced home. While most loan programs require a minimum 3% to 3.5% down payment, eligible borrowers may benefit from no-down-payment programs like those offered by the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) and the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA). If unsure, input the amount of money saved or potentially saved for a down payment, and remember that receiving a gift is also an option.

STEP 4: CHOOSE YOUR LOAN TERM

Your loan term represents the number of years required to fully repay your mortgage. Business Loan Warrior’s home affordability calculator considers a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage to determine the affordable house price, offering a lower and stable payment. However, opting for a 15-year fixed-rate term allows savings on interest, provided you can manage higher monthly payments. The trade-off is qualifying for a less costly home.

What the home affordability calculator results mean

A home affordability calculator tells you:

  1. The amount you could potentially afford is determined by the income and debt details you provide.
  2. The affordability is calculated based on estimated property taxes, homeowners insurance, and mortgage insurance (if applicable).
  3. How much you can afford based on whether you can qualify for mortgage financing

A home affordability calculator doesn’t tells you:

  1. Whether the lender will approve you for financing at the sales price shown
  2. What your final mortgage interest rate or closing costs will be
  3. How much your payment might vary based on your actual credit score

The bottom line:While the home affordability calculator provides an estimate of your potential qualification, it is recommended to obtain a mortgage preapproval for a more accurate dollar amount based on your individual financial situation.

MORTGAGE CALCULATOR TIP

Our calculator is pre-set to a “conservative” 28% DTI ratio. You can slide the bar up to an “aggressive” 50% DTI ratio to see how much more home you can buy. However, be sure your budget can handle the extra debt — lenders don’t look at expenses like utilities, car insurance, phone bills, home maintenance or groceries when they qualify you for a home loan. Lenders may also require a higher credit score, or extra mortgage reserves to cover a few month’s worth of mortgage payments, if the high payment becomes unaffordable.

5 ways to improve your home affordability

BOOST YOUR CREDIT SCORE

Your credit score evaluates your ability to handle credit based on your past and current credit management history.

The updated standard for obtaining the best rate on conventional mortgages is now set at 780, a 40-point increase from the previous benchmark of 740. To qualify for a lower interest rate and afford a higher-priced home, maintain low credit card balances, make timely payments, and avoid opening multiple new credit accounts.

MAX OUT YOUR MONTHLY INCOME

Having a cosigner can increase your borrowing power since two incomes are better than one. Additionally, don't overlook your side hustle income, as it can be considered if your tax returns demonstrate consistent part-time earnings over the past two years.

MAKE A BIGGER DOWN PAYMENT

To increase your home affordability, consider making a larger down payment. This reduces your loan amount and mortgage payment. Remember, the down payment doesn't have to come solely from your own funds – you can receive a gift from a relative, take a loan against your 401(k), or utilize down payment assistance programs.

REDUCE YOUR MONTHLY DEBT

Lenders assess both your current debt and the additional debt from your new mortgage payment. Having less debt gives you the ability to afford a higher-priced house.

Having a debt-to-income (DTI) ratio exceeding 40% may result in a higher conventional interest rate or increased closing costs starting from August 1.

CHOOSE A LONGER LOAN TERM

Opting for a longer repayment term, like 30 years, allows you to afford a larger home. However, choosing a shorter term can save you thousands in interest expenses, as long as the increased payment doesn't strain your monthly budget.

CONSIDER DIFFEERENT LOAN PROGRAMS

Government loan programs offer flexibility in extending your DTI ratio, even with a low credit score. However, they often entail higher mortgage insurance costs or guarantee fees, which may impact the amount you can afford.

How much mortgage can I afford based on my loan type?

How much house can I afford with a conventional loan?

Conventional loans are commonly chosen by borrowers with credit scores of 620 or higher and DTI ratios of 45% or less (with exceptions up to 50% DTI ratio). While some programs accept down payments as low as 3%, making a 20% down payment allows you to avoid mortgage insurance. However, conventional lenders typically require mortgage insurance as part of your monthly payment to protect against potential defaults.

How much house can I afford with an FHA loan?

First-time homebuyers who have faced challenges in their credit history often opt for Federal Housing Administration (FHA) insured loans. These loans allow borrowers with credit scores as low as 580 to qualify with a 3.5% down payment, while a score between 500 and 579 requires a minimum 10% down payment. However, a significant drawback of FHA loans is the requirement to pay mortgage insurance regardless of the down payment, which may limit your capacity to afford a higher-priced home.

How much house can I afford with a VA loan?

Military borrowers who meet the eligibility criteria often prefer VA loans due to their benefits, including no down payment requirement and no need for mortgage insurance. Although the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) does not set a minimum credit score, most VA-approved lenders prefer a score of at least 620. Additionally, VA borrowers may be required to pay a funding fee of up to 3.6% of the loan amount, unless they qualify for an exemption based on a military service-related disability.

How much house can I afford with a USDA loan?

Homebuyers with low to moderate income looking for homes in USDA-designated "rural" areas may be eligible for financing with no down payment. Typically, a minimum credit score of 640 is required, and instead of mortgage insurance, buyers pay an annual and upfront guarantee fee. However, strict income limits may restrict the amount of home you can purchase with a USDA loan, even if you meet the standard 41% debt-to-income (DTI) ratio requirement.

How much house can I afford according to my budget?

Understanding how much of your income remains after covering monthly expenses provides insight into your affordability for a house. While a mortgage lender can determine your affordability based on minimum requirements, relying solely on these rules may not be advisable for various reasons:

Lenders primarily rely on your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio, which compares your total debt to your pre-tax earnings, to determine home affordability. However, they often overlook important factors such as your take-home pay and recurring expenses like cellphone bills, insurance, groceries, and entertainment. It's crucial to ensure that your loan approval leaves room for your lifestyle, as you don't want to compromise your financial well-being for monthly obligations.

The 28/36 rule

Financial planners frequently refer to the 28/36 rule as a guideline for determining home affordability. The 28 signifies the recommended debt-to-income (DTI) ratio for your mortgage payment in relation to your gross income, known as the "front-end" DTI ratio. The 36 represents your total monthly debt obligations, including your mortgage payment and other debts like car loans, credit cards, student loans, and other accounts listed on your credit report, referred to as the "back-end" DTI ratio in lender terminology.

Here’s an example of how it works, assuming you make $6,250 per month ($75,000 per year).

Q:What other factors affect home affordability?

Additional things that can impact how much house you can afford include:
Property taxes. Tax rates in some states are significantly higher than others. Expect to pay more if you live in a city.
Homeowners association or condo fees. Buying a home in an HOA or condominium complex may come with hefty monthly fees to cover all the amenities you can enjoy in these types of properties. The extra cost is counted against your DTI ratio, which reduces how much you qualify for.
The condition of the home you’re buying. If you’re thinking about buying a fixer-upper, make sure you have an idea of how much money you’ll need to spend to get the home move-in ready. Consider a fixer-upper loan: You can buy the home and roll the improvement costs into one loan.

Q:Should I buy now if rates are high?

Timing the real estate market is like timing the stock market — it can rise and fall due to unexpected forces outside of your control. If you’re comfortable with the monthly payment and plan to live in the home for several years, buying is usually a good financial investment.

Q:How does PITI affect home affordability?

Lenders use a principal, interest, taxes and insurance (PITI) figure to calculate your debt-to-income ratio. The insurance component may include mortgage insurance, which is usually paid as part of your monthly payment. The higher your PITI payment, the less you can afford.
